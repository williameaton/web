About me

I am a graduate student in the Theoretical and Computational seismology at Princeton University, having completed an integrated Bachelors and Masters degree in Earth Sciences at the University of Oxford, UK. My research interests are in geophysical numerical modelling, predominantly in seismology and geodynamics.

During the pandemic, I co-founded the TETHYS seminar series. This project grew out of a passion to increase the opportunities for young geoscientists to network and give scientific presentations within a more informal setting. This project ran for 3 series, with a total of 28 speakers from 12 different universities spanning North America, Europe and Australia.

Outside of academia my heart lies in exploring nature, watching Liverpool FC and drinking tea.

Research

Modelling gravity with SPECFEM-X

Supervised by Jeroen Tromp (Princeton University), in collaboration with Hom Nath Gharti (Queen's University)

Modelling Earth's gravitational potential field is of significant interest for both geodynamic forward and inverse problems. The fact that potential fields, unlike other fields such as displacement, are non-zero outside of a body has provided a challenge for numerical modelling as infinite space can not be meshed. This has placed limitations on numerical modelling of geodynamic processes occurring over long timescales which require consideration of gravitational forces.
SPECFEM-X, recently developed by Hom Nath Gharti and Jeroen Tromp, incorporates gravitational potential fields into SPECFEM-GLOBE using a spectral infinite-element method to solve the Laplace equation in an unbounded spatial domain (see Gharti et al 2018). This refinement enables modelling of 3D quasi-static and elastic problems involving all the features of SPECFEM-GLOBE, but with the additional capability to accurately model spatio-temporal changes to the gravitational field.
My current research involves benchmarking SPECFEM-X and utilising this new toolkit to simulate Earth's nutations and wobbles, as well as visco-elastic modelling of sea-level change.

Seismic scattering and wavefield diffusivity

Supervised by Tarje Nissen-Meyer (University of Oxford)

Seismic-wave scattering is observed, to variable degrees of strength, on all planetary bodies for which seismic records exist (e.g. from the InSight and Apollo missions). Global-scale imaging using scattered wavefields is a relatively juvenile topic within seismology, but it is capable of resolving structure orders-of-magnitude smaller than is possible using traditional inversion methods such as tomography. Current scattering models and analytical methods are limited to two end-member phenomena: weak single- or multiple-scattering events (ballistic), or intense scattering such that the wavefield retains no phase information (diffuse). My research explores the existence of scattering behaviour intermediate between these end-members, and the dominant properties of heterogeneous media that facilitate a transition between them. We use novel analytical techniques such as multi-scale entropy and frequency-correlation analysis. Combining these newly-developed techniques with traditional analytical methods provides a promising framework for future exploration of scattered wavefields across ballistic, transitional, and diffuse regimes. The analytical methods developed are also being used in consideration of nuclear bomb test monitoring.

AxiSEM-Shapes

AxiSEM-Shapes is a series of python scripts used to inject 3D shapes such as cylinders, ellipsoids and slabs into 3D models for AxiSEM-3D. Examples for use can be found as part of the AxiSEM3D package.

The newest implementation works for both cartesian and global (spherical/ellipsoidal) simulations, though there are some bugs for high-latitude locations which need fixing.

Correlation-Coefficients

This repo calculates 'Correlation Coefficient' matrices following the method described in Liu and Ben-Zion (2016), which I utilise in the study of planetary coda.

SPECFEM-C

SPECFEM-C is a project under development to incorporate a rate-dependent formulation of the Sea Level equations into SPECFEM, utilising the spectral-infinite-element method. The goal is to make the package open-source for sea-level change related to Glacio-isostatic adjustment. The above figure shows an exemplar global Spectral-infinite-element mesh used in this code.

SPECFEM Website

Following a recent meeting of SPECFEM developers in Toronto, October 2022, a dedicated website has been setup for the family of SPECFEM codes. Details on the theory of SPECFEM, documentation, training resources and publications are all being added. Included within the host repository are resources to automatically download citations from Google Scholar for a specific user. Please get in contact if you are interested in using these scripts! We welcome and encourage any contributions to the SPECFEM website or code family.

TRModes

TRModes is a toolkit for calculating and visualising Toroidal and Radial normal modes of an arbitrary, spherically-symmetric sphere (e.g. Earth). It harnesses PyVista and Paraview to allow interactive 3D animations of an oscillatory body.

This was developed in collaboration with Huda Al Alawi, Page Dabney and Terry Schuh. TRModes is still under construction, with some known bugs. We aim to impliment a GUI and Spheroidal modes in the future.
